    approach and reframes success entirely.

    “You do not rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your systems.” 
  </div>

  <p>
    Goals alone do not transform you. <span class="highlight">Systems do</span> — small daily actions that require minimal
    motivation yet produce maximum impact.
  </p>

  <h2>The Core Idea: The Power of 1% Improvement </h2>

  <p>
    One of the most compelling insights from the book is the concept of becoming just <span class="highlight">1% better each day</span>.
  </p>

  <ul>
    <li>Improve 1% daily → you become <span class="highlight">37 times better</span> in one year.</li>
    <li>Decline 1% daily → you drop dangerously close to zero.</li>
  </ul>

  <p>
    Habits compound just like financial interest. Tiny actions that seem insignificant in the moment can completely change
    your life over time.
  </p>

  <h3>Examples of Tiny Habits with Big Results</h3>

  <ul>
    <li> Reading 1 page a day → 12–15 books a year</li>
    <li> Jogging 5 minutes a day → builds a fitness routine</li>
    <li> Saving ₹10 a day → strengthens financial discipline</li>
    <li> Studying 10 minutes daily → boosts academic consistency</li>
  </ul>

  <p>
    Small habits shape your <span class="highlight">identity</span>, and identity shapes your <span class="highlight">future</span>.
  </p>

  <!-- Four Laws -->
  <h2>The Four Laws of Behavior Change</h2>

  <p>
    James Clear introduces a science-backed framework that simplifies habit building into four actionable laws.
  </p>

  <!-- 1. Make It Obvious -->
  <h3>Make It Obvious</h3>

  <p>
    Good habits require clear cues. Bad habits thrive because triggers are everywhere.
  </p>

  <h4>Practical Examples</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>Keeping a book on your pillow → encourages nighttime reading</li>
    <li>Placing shoes near the door → promotes morning walks</li>
    <li>Removing junk food from your desk → reduces mindless eating</li>
  </ul>

  <h4>Habit Stacking</h4>

  <div class="quote">
    “After I do X, I will do Y.”
  </div>

  <p>
    Example:<br>
    <strong>After brushing my teeth → I will meditate for 2 minutes.</strong>
  </p>

  <!-- 2. Make It Attractive -->
  <h3> Make It Attractive</h3>

  <p>
    We naturally repeat what feels rewarding. To build a new habit, associate it with something enjoyable. 
  </p>

  <p>
    Example:<br>
    Listen to your favorite music only when you exercise — soon the workout itself becomes appealing.
  </p>

  <!-- 3. Make It Easy -->
  <h3> Make It Easy</h3>

  <p>
    Bad habits feel easy. Good habits feel difficult. So, reduce friction. Simplify the process. Minimize effort.
  </p>

  <h4>Examples</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>Want to study? → Keep your books open on your table</li>
    <li>Want to eat healthy? → Prepare fruits in advance</li>
    <li>Want to reduce screen time? → Keep your phone in another room</li>
  </ul>

  <p>
    The easier the action, the more sustainable the habit becomes.
  </p>

  <!-- 4. Make It Satisfying -->
  <h3> Make It Satisfying</h3>

  <p>
    We repeat behaviors that give immediate rewards. Therefore, ensure your habit feels satisfying at the end. 
  </p>

  <h4>Ways to Reinforce Satisfaction</h4>

  <ul>
    <li>Track your progress in a journal</li>
    <li>Use a digital habit tracker</li>
    <li>Give yourself small rewards after consistent practice</li>
  </ul>

  <p>
    Your brain thrives on positive reinforcement — use it strategically.
  </p>

  <!-- Why People Fail -->
  <h2>Why People Fail — and How <em>Atomic Habits</em> Solves It</h2>

  <p>
    Many people start with good intentions but eventually fail. James Clear explains the hidden reasons behind this.
  </p>

  <ul>
    <li><span class="highlight">Trying to change everything at once</span> → Start with one small habit.</li>
    <li><span class="highlight">Relying on motivation</span> → Build systems, not emotional dependency.</li>
    <li><span class="highlight">Focusing on goals instead of identity</span> → True behavior change comes from identity.</li>
    <li><span class="highlight">Giving up after one failure</span> → “Never miss twice.”</li>
  </ul>

  <p>
    Identity-based habits create lasting transformation.
  </p>
